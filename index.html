Overview

“the innate tendency to focus on life and lifelike processes” E.O. Wilson (1986)

“a person is a biological organism, a social being, and a unique individual” Y.-F. Tuan (1990)

Do we need to add a people dimension to the existing biophilic design dimensions of nature and place?

  • The Biophilia Hypothesis suggests that the human-nature connect is ‘innate’ i.e. genetic/biological. However humans also have individual characteristics. Could these individual characteristics be significant when considering human-nature connection?
  • Research question: “To what extent does individual relationship with nature influence psychological and physiological response to nature exposure?”

Meta-analyses

Emotion, Gaekwad et al 2022

Physiological stress, Gaekwad et al 2023

  • Previous work (clickable links) were meta-analyses considering emotion (biophilia) and stress (Stress Recovery Theory) respectively.
  • Used to evidence the effect of humans on nature, and design the main study.
  • Attention was also considered, but there was already a meta-analysis of sufficient quality published. Less work for me :)

Laboratory Study

Sixty-one participants

Exposure to images of ‘urban’ and ‘natural’ environments in SE Australia

  • Within-subjects laboratory experiment exposing 61 participants to nature and urban image scenes
  • Use of a stressor (Trail Making Test B)
  • Images are ‘typical’ of natural and urban areas able to be accessed by someone living in Melbourne or Geelong
  • Images were rated on a custom aesthetic scale based on restorative environment theory. The most and least restorative environments were selected for natural and urban scenes respectively.

For simplicity, no people were included in the images. This was a difficult measure to accomplish for the urban scenes. However, the COVID-19 lockdowns presented a rare opportunity.

  • Natural environments included trees, bushland, and areas with water.
  • It is interesting to note that ‘nature’ is less ‘green’ and less forested than common Northern Hemisphere environments.

Laboratory study

Collection of rich quantitative & qualitative data:

  • demographics & nature connection
  • emotion, attention, stress
  • nature experiences
  • gaze (eye-tracking)

Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (Watson et al. 1988)

  • Collection of quantitative (emotion, attention, stress), qualitative (critical incident method), and gaze (eye tracking) data
  • Data analysis is still in progress, this presentation contains preliminary results for emotion only.
  • PANAS is a 20 item state affect scale with 10 items generally considering positive affect and 10 items generally considering negative affect). Combined valence and arousal through weighting of each item//emotion.
  • Affect is a ‘simplification’ of more complex emotions. Affect considers valence (positive or negative) and arousal (weak or strong). Emotion is a more complex construct.
  • Affect is an important outcome variable as the Biophilia Hypothesis posits an emotional connection with nature.

Results

  • Mixed-model (within/between) ANOVA with group x environment interaction
  • Discard small group sizes if ANOVA assumptions (e.g. equal variances) unable to be met
  • Displayed results are p < .05 and power 80%. These are ‘typical’ Type I and II error probabilities in psychological research.

ηp2 = 0.29

  • CNS is the Connectedness to Nature Scale. The participants were split into three groups (low-blue, medium-orange, high-green).
  • The outcome variable is Positive Affect
  • Response to nature is on the left, urban is on the right
  • A result of 0 indicates that the environment had no effect. This is roughly seen for nature on the left for all CNS groups.
  • However, the low CNS group has a much poorer response to the urban environment (i.e. the urban environment reduces their positive affect)
  • These results suggest that differences in individual connection to nature do influence human response to nature!

ηp2 = 0.30

  • Similar graph setup to the previous slide, however the individual characteristic here is how often participants spent time in nature during their childhood.
  • Participants with less frequent (monthly) self-reported childhood exposure had a more negative response to the urban environment.

ηp2 = 0.12

  • Similar to the previous two, however both the outcome variable and the individual characteristic are different here.
  • The individual characteristic is a question contributing to ‘life stress’ - how often a participant felt they were unable to control the important things in their life.
  • The outcome variable is ‘Irritability’
  • As previous, a 0 means that the environment had no effect
  • The results indicate that people with a higher life stress gain a greater benefit from nature i.e. the natural environment reduces their irritability more. Similarly, the urban environment makes them more irritable.
  • People with a lower life stress have similar responses to both natural and urban environments - both make them a little bit irritable! This could be due to the duration of the environment image display (10 mins) or the duration of the study (nominally 60 minutes but up to 100 minutes for some participants).
  • The magnitude of the effect here is much smaller than the previous results. This begs the question - does this statistical effect have any practical effect?

The data suggest that individual characteristics do weakly influence the effects of nature

Urban scenes have a greater negative effect on individuals with:

  • lower connection to nature
  • higher life stress
  • There is evidence to indicate that individual characteristics, including long-term stress state and connection to nature, can influence the effects of nature.
  • Data analysis is incomplete, but suggests that this individual effect is weak - this conclusion would correspond to the biophilia hypothesis and its biological basis.
  • Results indicate that lower connection to nature results in a higher reduction of positive affect from urban scenes
  • Similar with higher life stress and higher reduction of positive effect from urban scenes
  • Do we need to account for a human dimension to nature-based design?

Limitations

  • Individuals with lower connection to nature are a minority
  • Lossy acquisition of some physiological data
  • Laboratory rather than immersion
  • Non-extreme environments
  • Sample size is decent, however more homogeneous than I would have liked - need to significantly oversample to represent minorities (e.g. lower connection with nature).
  • A quantitative experimental method may not be suitable for studying such a minority in the future - other methods (e.g. deeper qualitative methods such as longitudinal studies) should be considered for future research.
  • I’m also working with significant data loss:
    • Pulse signal data loss (HR, HRV) in both time and frequency domains due to shortcomings in the Empatica E4 sensor for this sort of research work.
    • Eye tracking due to people having corrective lenses, and the difficulty of accounting for head movement.
    • I’m currently using Python image processing and machine learning to tackle this - it is a work in progress. Please reach out to me if you’re interested in this work - might present at a conference in the future.
    • Still exploring the significance of what a point or half point difference in the results mean. Statistically significant, yes, but any significant effect on well-being? This is a large and probably difficult question to solve.
